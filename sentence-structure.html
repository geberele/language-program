Grammar

Sentence structure

English is a Subject–Verb–Object language. Master that backbone and the rest — adjectives, adverbs, clauses — fit around it in predictable places.

The basic pattern

Maria (subject) writes (verb) letters (object).

Changing the order changes the meaning — or breaks the sentence entirely. Italian is flexible here; English is not.

Typical English word order

A full sentence tends to follow this order:

Subject → Verb → Object → Manner → Place → Time
She (S) ate (V) dinner (O) quickly (M) at home (P) yesterday (T).

Four sentence types

  • Simple — one independent clause. The cat sleeps.
  • Compound — two independent clauses joined by a conjunction. The cat sleeps and the dog plays.
  • Complex — one independent + one dependent clause. The cat sleeps because it's tired.
  • Compound-complex — two independent + at least one dependent. The cat sleeps because it's tired, but the dog keeps playing.

Questions and negatives

English uses the auxiliary do/does/did to form questions and negatives with most verbs.

Do you speak English?

She doesn't like coffee.

With be, modals, and perfect forms, invert directly — no do needed.

Are you tired? / Can he swim? / Have they arrived?

Adjective order before a noun

When stacking adjectives, English follows a strict order. Getting it wrong sounds "off" even when grammatical.

Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material → Purpose → Noun
A lovely little old round red Italian wooden dining table.
