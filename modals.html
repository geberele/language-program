Verbs & Tenses

Modal verbs

Modals add attitude to a verb — possibility, obligation, permission, advice. They never take -s, and the verb after them stays in the base form.

Rules that apply to all modals

  • Followed directly by the base verb: she can swim, not can to swim.
  • No -s in the third person: he must go, not musts.
  • Questions and negatives are formed without do: Can you help? / She cannot come.

The core modals

willFuture, willingness, promisesI will help you.
wouldPolite requests, hypotheticals, past habitsI would travel if I could.
ought toAdvice (like should)You ought to apologize.

Semi-modals

These behave like modals in meaning but follow regular verb rules.

  • have to — external obligation. I have to work on Saturday.
  • need to — necessity. You need to call the office.
  • be able to — ability in any tense. She was able to finish on time.
  • used to — past habit or state. I used to live in Turin.
must vs. have to. Must often feels internal (you decide); have to feels external (someone else decides). In the past, we almost always use had to.
