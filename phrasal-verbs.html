Phrasal verbs

A phrasal verb is a verb + a particle (preposition or adverb) whose combined meaning is often different from the verb alone. Give up ≠ give. They're the backbone of everyday spoken English — and a major reason native speech can feel opaque.

Quick rules

  • Separable phrasal verbs let the object go between the verb and particle: turn the light off or turn off the light. If the object is a pronoun, it must go in the middle: turn it off, not turn off it.
  • Inseparable phrasal verbs keep the parts together: look after the kids, not look the kids after.
  • Intransitive phrasal verbs take no object: the plane took off.
  • Many phrasal verbs have multiple meanings — pick up can mean collect, learn, or answer a phone. Context decides.
