Grammar

Adjectives

Adjectives describe nouns. In English they don't change for gender or number — one red apple, two red apples. That's the easy part. The tricky part is order and comparison.

Where they go

  • Before a noun: a tall building.
  • After a linking verb (be, seem, look, feel, become): the building is tall.

Order of adjectives

When you stack multiple adjectives before a noun, English insists on this order:

OSASCOMP: Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material → Purpose

A beautiful small old round black Italian leather riding boot.

Comparison patterns

  • as … as — equality. She is as tall as her brother.
  • not as … as — inequality. Today isn't as cold as yesterday.
  • the + superlative. This is the best coffee in Rome.
  • than after comparatives. Rome is older than Milan.

-ed vs. -ing adjectives

A common confusion: -ed describes how you feel; -ing describes the thing causing the feeling.

I'm bored. (my feeling)

The lesson is boring. (what causes it)

Rule of thumb. People usually take -ed; things usually take -ing.
